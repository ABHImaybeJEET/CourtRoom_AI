On January 7, 2009, Ramalinga Raju, the Chairman of Satyam Computer Services, a leading Indian IT firm, confessed that the company's accounts had been falsified. During a trial simulating the aftermath of the Satyam Computer Services scandal, the prosecution alleged that Raju and his inner circle systemically fabricated revenues, margins, and over 5,000 crore INR (approximately $1 billion USD) in fictitious cash balances to the board, investors, and auditors over several years. The defense argued that Raju did not personally siphon the money for malicious self-enrichment, but was caught riding a "tiger, not knowing how to get off without being eaten," initially inflating numbers to conceal poor operational performance, which then spiraled out of control.

The prosecution aims to prove criminal conspiracy, cheating, and forgery under the Indian Penal Code (IPC) and violations of the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) regulations, seeking maximum financial penalties and imprisonment. The defense counters that the systemic failure of the external auditors (PwC) mitigates the board's direct criminal intent to defraud, arguing for a more lenient sentence focusing on corporate mismanagement rather than organized criminal looting.
